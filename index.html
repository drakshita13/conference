When Bilateral Effusion Isn't Malignancy:
A Rare Case of Systemic Amyloidosis

Advanced Diagnostic Case Study in Respiratory Medicine

Dr. Akshita Khare
MD Resident, Pulmonary Medicine (2nd Year)
Shri Aurobindo Institute of Medical Sciences
Department of Respiratory Medicine, Indore
01

Background

Clinical Context & Diagnostic Challenge

Bilateral pleural effusion in adults most commonly arises from malignancy, tuberculosis, or congestive cardiac failure. In the absence of fever or infective markers, diagnostic focus shifts towards neoplastic or systemic causes.
Amyloidosis, a rare disorder characterized by extracellular deposition of misfolded amyloid fibrils, is seldom considered early in the differential diagnosis. Pleural involvement in amyloidosis is unusual and often mimics more prevalent pathologies, leading to significant diagnostic delays.
Key Clinical Challenge
This case exemplifies the diagnostic challenge posed by rare systemic diseases presenting with common clinical manifestations, emphasizing the critical importance of systematic evaluation when initial investigations remain inconclusive.
02

Case Presentation

Patient Profile & Clinical Features

Patient Profile

Adult male with progressive exertional dyspnea of 6 months duration, accompanied by bilateral pleural effusion with left-sided predominance, anorexia, and significant weight loss.

Clinical Presentation
  • Progressive exertional dyspnea (6-month duration)
  • Bilateral pleural effusion (left > right)
  • Anorexia with unintentional weight loss
  • Chronic, insidious onset
Notable Absence
  • No fever or constitutional symptoms
  • No chest pain or pleuritic symptoms
  • No hemoptysis or productive cough
  • No orthopnea or paroxysmal dyspnea
Previous Evaluation
  • Multiple diagnostic thoracocenteses performed
  • Transudative pleural fluid obtained
  • Cytological analysis: inconclusive results
  • Temporary symptomatic relief only
03

Diagnostic Journey

Sequential Investigation & Key Findings

Diagnostic Challenge
Recurrent bilateral pleural effusion with non-diagnostic pleural fluid analysis in the absence of obvious malignancy or cardiac failure necessitated comprehensive systemic evaluation.
Chest HRCT
Confirmed bilateral pleural effusions without evidence of parenchymal lesions, mediastinal lymphadenopathy, or obvious malignant process
2D Echocardiography
Revealed characteristic features of restrictive cardiomyopathy with preserved ejection fraction but impaired diastolic function
Cardiac MRI
Demonstrated diffuse myocardial infiltration with characteristic enhancement patterns highly suggestive of infiltrative cardiomyopathy
Definitive Diagnosis
Comprehensive hematological and histopathological evaluation confirmed systemic AL amyloidosis with cardiac and pleural involvement
04

Clinical Discussion

Pathophysiology & Diagnostic Insights

In contemporary respiratory medicine practice, recurrent bilateral pleural effusion typically suggests malignancy, tubercular etiology, or cardiac dysfunction. Amyloidosis represents a rare but critical differential diagnosis, particularly when routine cytological and biochemical analyses remain inconclusive despite repeated evaluations.
Pathophysiological Mechanism
Pleural effusion in amyloidosis results from direct pleural amyloid infiltration, impaired lymphatic drainage, or secondary to restrictive cardiomyopathy with elevated filling pressures.
This case demonstrates the pivotal role of advanced cardiac imaging modalities, particularly cardiac MRI, in identifying infiltrative diseases. The systematic approach to diagnosis underscores the necessity of maintaining a comprehensive differential diagnosis in atypical presentations of common clinical syndromes.
05

Clinical Pearls

Key Learning Points

💎 Essential Clinical Insights
Consider amyloidosis in unexplained bilateral pleural effusion when common causes are excluded
Cardiac MRI provides crucial diagnostic insights in suspected infiltrative cardiomyopathy
Multidisciplinary evaluation is essential for rare systemic diseases
Early recognition significantly impacts therapeutic outcomes and prognosis
Systematic persistence in diagnostic evaluation leads to accurate diagnosis in complex cases

Clinical Conclusion

Systemic amyloidosis must be considered in the differential diagnosis of non-resolving bilateral pleural effusion, particularly when infectious and malignant etiologies have been systematically excluded. Early recognition through multidisciplinary evaluation and advanced imaging is crucial, as diagnostic delays significantly impact prognosis in amyloidosis with cardiac involvement.

Diagnostic Vigilance
Maintain comprehensive differential diagnosis when common causes are systematically excluded
Advanced Imaging Utility
Cardiac MRI is indispensable for detecting infiltrative cardiomyopathy in suspected systemic diseases
Early Recognition Impact
Timely diagnosis enables appropriate subspecialty management and significantly improves patient outcomes
Multidisciplinary Approach
Complex systemic diseases require collaborative evaluation involving multiple medical specialties
