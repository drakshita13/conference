When Bilateral Effusion Isn’t Malignancy

A Rare Case of Systemic Amyloidosis
Dr. Akshita Khare — MD (Respiratory Medicine), 2nd Year Resident • Department of Respiratory Medicine & TB, SAIMS, Indore • akhare13@gmail.com

Abstract

Background: Bilateral pleural effusion is commonly due to malignancy, tuberculosis, or congestive cardiac failure. In afebrile patients with recurrent, non‑diagnostic pleural fluid, rare systemic causes such as systemic amyloidosis must be considered. Amyloidosis is an infiltrative disorder characterized by extracellular deposition of misfolded amyloid fibrils and may involve pleura and myocardium.

Case summary: A —year-old male with progressive exertional dyspnea (5–6 months), anorexia, and weight loss presented with bilateral pleural effusion (L>R). Multiple outside thoracocenteses were non-diagnostic. At our center, HRCT chest excluded parenchymal mass; 2D-ECHO suggested restrictive physiology and cardiac MRI revealed diffuse myocardial infiltration. Targeted testing confirmed systemic amyloidosis with cardiac and pleural involvement.

Key message: Consider infiltrative systemic disease (amyloidosis) in persistent, afebrile bilateral pleural effusions with inconclusive cytology.
Keywords: bilateral pleural effusion · systemic amyloidosis · restrictive cardiomyopathy · cardiac MRI · diagnostic reasoning

Case Presentation

Presenting complaint: Progressive dyspnea on exertion (5–6 months); bilateral pleural effusions (L>R); anorexia and weight loss; afebrile.

Prior care: Multiple diagnostic thoracocenteses performed elsewhere — non-diagnostic cytology; symptomatic therapy with persistent symptoms.

Examination: Dullness to percussion and reduced breath sounds at bilateral bases (L>R); clinically hemodynamically stable.

Clinical cue: Afebrile + recurrent bilateral effusion + negative cytology → broaden differential beyond TB/malignancy.

Investigations & Findings

Imaging

  • HRCT Chest: bilateral pleural effusions; no parenchymal mass or mediastinal lymphadenopathy.
  • 2D-Echocardiography: features suggestive of restrictive cardiomyopathy.
  • Cardiac MRI: diffuse myocardial infiltration consistent with an infiltrative cardiomyopathy (amyloidosis suspected).

Pleural fluid & Labs

  • Pleural fluid: repeated taps — non-diagnostic cytology; cultures/ADA not suggestive of TB.
  • Serum/urine protein electrophoresis ± immunofixation; serum free light chains recommended.
  • Tissue biopsy (fat pad/pleura/endomyocardial): Congo red & apple‑green birefringence confirms amyloid.

Differential Diagnosis

1. Malignancy-related effusion
2. Tuberculous pleural effusion
3. Heart failure–related effusion
4. Pulmonary embolism
5. Systemic amyloidosis

Discussion

Diagnostic reasoning: Initial pre-test probability favored malignancy given weight loss and bilateral effusion, but repeated non-diagnostic cytology and lack of mass on HRCT redirected the workup. Echocardiography and cardiac MRI were decisive in identifying an infiltrative process and prioritized amyloidosis-specific testing.

Mechanisms: Effusions in amyloidosis arise from pleural infiltration, impaired lymphatic drainage, myocardial dysfunction causing elevated venous pressures, or hypoalbuminemia.

Clinical implication: Early identification of cardiac involvement (via MRI/echo) alters prognosis and treatment strategies; typing the amyloid (AL vs ATTR) is essential for therapy selection.

Conclusion

Not every bilateral effusion is TB or malignancy. In afebrile, persistent bilateral pleural effusions with inconclusive cytology, systemic amyloidosis should be actively considered. An imaging-led, multidisciplinary approach prevents diagnostic delay and enables targeted therapy planning.

Learning Points

  • Consider infiltrative systemic disease in recurrent, afebrile bilateral pleural effusions.
  • Cardiac MRI is high-yield when echocardiography suggests restrictive physiology.
  • Tissue diagnosis (Congo red) remains the gold standard for amyloid detection.
  • Coordinate early multidisciplinary input (Respiratory, Cardiology, Pathology).
